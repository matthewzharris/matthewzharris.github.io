Ready Seattle

Mobile App Design

Develop a new mobile app that would help prepare Seattle residents for natural disasters.

Overview

Project Brief: They were seeking our help to create a mobile app that will help bring the information from their website into the phones of Seattle residents.

Client

Seattle Emergency Management

Tools

Pen/Paper, Omnigraffle, Sketch, Keynote

Timeframe

2 Weeks

Deliverables

Final High Fidelity Prototype

Team

3 UX Designers

Role

Information Architect, Visual Designer, Project Manager

My Impact

During this concept project, I was lead in information architecture and visual design as well as the project manager of our UX design team. I did the business research of the project through a comparative analysis of features in the disaster app market and a heuristic analysis of top apps. I created the current sitemap and mobile sitemap, hi fidelity prototypes, and the Ready Seattle branding. I also lead the presentation of design solutions.

Research

Comparative Analysis

I lead the comparative analysis of disaster prep apps in the market so that we could review potential features. The features we found were:

  • Alerts
  • Emergency material checklist
  • Natural disaster tips
  • Multi Language support
  • Filters for alerts

Heuristic Analysis

I did heuristic analysis on the best app that helped prepare and inform a local citizenry, Ready NYC.

The app was good at:

  • Organizational Clarity - IA
  • Supporting Readability - Fonts used are easy to read
  • Facilitating User Tasks - Plan page are straightforward with tasks

Some improvement was necessary to fix:

  • Unclear Labeling - labelling is unclear in the screens themselves
  • Inconsistent/Ineffective Design - Not much visual design at all, color panes change every screen, zero branding

User Research

We interviewed 5 Seattle residents about natural disasters.

From the research the common issues were:

  • The only disaster education was in early schooling
  • The plan for emergencies is to just “Google it”
  • Little to no previous experience with natural disasters
  • Preparing for an emergency felt low urgency

Persona

Context Scenario: Jake and his girlfriend live in an apartment in Capitol Hill, Seattle. Jake and his co-workers have been talking about the news regarding an upcoming big earthquake that is predicted to hit Seattle in the near future. He has never experienced any major natural disaster in his life. After the conversation with his coworkers, he felt very unprepared about any emergency situations that he might encounter. He decides to come up with an emergency plan and prepare emergency kits for both him and his girlfriend.

Problem

Seattle’s millennials don’t consider being prepared for a natural disaster an important or easily actionable problem.

Goal

To make a mobile app that simplifies disaster preparation and disaster information in order to increase the readiness of Seattle residents by 6%

Paper Prototype

We created a paper prototype to help create an Emergency Go Bag, provide emergency information, and alerts.

From usability testing, we found:

  • Itemized list view within “Go Bag” tab confused users
  • Go Bag screen needs more motivation

Low Fidelity Wireframes

In a design studio, we developed a new Go Bag screen with a visual that would show what items have been added and what percentage the user has collected.

We wanted to focus on the user motivation for completing tasks by giving them a success metric and indicating their progress.

Elements that needed improvement were:

  • Map screen pins unidentified
  • Alerts not clickable
  • Info page and categories within unclear
  • Term “Go Bag” not always intuitive

Solution

Visual Design

Since ReadySeattle didn’t previously exist, I started from scratch to create a brand. For the primary logo, I was inspired by a disaster prep infographic of an emergency bag from our client. The go bag is a universal image of emergency readiness which was fitting for our app vision. The color palette was chosen by researching earthquake alert colors and then choosing slightly brighter shades to be appealing to users. Readability was a big factor so I used Apple’s San Francisco typeface based on their Human Interface Guidelines (HIG). The iconography for the Go Bag items was influenced by icons from our client but were updated with the ReadySeattle colors.The navigation icons were designed with simplicity in mind to help the go bag icons stand out on the screen.

High Fidelity Wireframes

With our style guide complete, I created our loading screen and emergency alert notification modal. For usability tests, we went through 3 major flows through the app.

After entering, the user lands immediately on the Alerts page where view alerts and the time it was issued. To respond to user feedback, arrows let users know its clickable to see additional alert details. They can click the hyperlink I created to see the alert on the map. In response to users, I created a legend on the map to provide context for the pins.

The Go Bag flow is where users collect materials for emergencies. Here, I added a description of the Go Bag to help inform our users of its meaning. I also provided them with a responsive visualization as they added items to their Go Bag as the bag is filled it will look more like the final app logo.

For the final flow about emergency resources, I renamed the “Info” page to “Resources” based on where users were navigating for natural disaster information.

Testing Results

We received the following final usability test findings:

  • Users expected a back button on Go Bag and Alert screens
  • Upon first visit, the Show Current Bag button should be grayed out
  • Alerts in feed need to be more specific in title
  • Visualize disasters on map as shaded regions

What I Learned

  • Communication across team members is mission critical. Any insight lost from researcher to final visual designer is a lost opportunity to respond to users.
  • Gathering initial user research from differing demographics is crucial because those things will influence your persona and final design
  • Testing with users is incredibly important because their responses to your design is almost always met differently than expected
