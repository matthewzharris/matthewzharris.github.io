Cue Reminder

Mobile App Redesign

Easily schedule casual social events, chat with attendees, and share ETA

Overview

Project Brief: B Tech Global created a mobile app to schedule events, share your estimated time arrival and allow chat for events. B Tech Global was eager to improve the user experience and usability testing results and help further define Cue’s brand and vision.

Client

B Tech Global

Tools

Pen & Paper, Sketch, Omnigraffle, Keynote

Timeframe

2.5 weeks

Deliverables

Interactive Final Prototype, Redlines, Annotations, Presentation

Team

2 Designers

Role

Information Architect, Interaction Design, Visual Designer

Design Process

My Impact

During this client project, I was the lead on information architecture, interaction design and visual design. I led the business analysis research in terms of heuristic analysis of the current product, comparative analysis, and feature comparison in the market. I led the initial sketches, led a design studio, developed the current and future state sitemaps, user flows, low and high fidelity prototypes. I also redesigned the Cue branding for the project.

Research

Comparative Analysis

I led our business analysis research of the event mobile app market to see where there might be opportunities for Cue.

  • For informal events, texts and group texts were used to create events, even though there were significant issues with group texts like non-responses, general confusion with no specific place for event details, etc.
  • For formal events, people typically use a calendar app to schedule meetings (more for work environments) and used Facebook events for more formal scheduled events with friends.
From this analysis, we determined that their might be an opportunity to help create informal events with more structure than texting.

Heuristic Analysis

I lead a heuristic analysis of the existing Cue mobile app and noted some elements to be updated:

  • Outdated, inconsistent, and distracting visual elements (for instance, background image on the main event screen)
  • Unintuitive user flows, specifically when it comes to navigation (for example, getting to an event chat took multiple screens)
  • Issues with the language used caused privacy concerns

User Research

We performed contextual inquiries to test usability of the current MVP. The majority of the findings were around the interface not being intuitive and lack of feedback for users completing tasks.

The user researcher performed 5 user interviews with potential Cue users in order to discover their process for scheduling events. We then pulled quotes from the interviews and created an affinity diagram so that we could uncover some common themes and pain points. The two main common themes were:

  • Problems with texting include requiring excessive back-and-forth and ambiguity surrounding commitment
  • Problems with more formal platforms include needing all parties to have that application and needing many, well defined details

Persona

From research, we created Jill Smith who is a busy working mom with packed work and family schedules, but she still tries to see her friends whenever they have free time. She hates when people are late, getting e-vites, and being the go-between for events. Her pain points with events are that she and her friends often forget to meet when they planned because of their busy lives. They also often need to change when and where they planned to meet and coordinate between multiple friends. With Jill in mind, we created our problem to design for.

Problem

People with busy calendars, who feel concerned about managing casual social events, face complex and formal barriers in existing forms of event scheduling.

Goal

To create a mobile app that can provide a quick and easy way to create informal social events

Ideation

Initial Design

After creating a list of features to respond to Jill, we used the MoSCoW method to come up with the following feature prioritization that helped to define our vision for Cue.

After initial sketches, I led a design studio to brainstorm the flow of information and interface of our features. I wanted to explore how to be “chat first” (something that the client saw as important) while maintaining the primary focus on creating/managing events.

Paper Prototype

We then created a paper prototype to test the designs of the high-level information architecture and user flows.

From usability testing, we found:

  • Event creation screen language confusing
  • Unsure if event was created
  • Tab bar doesn’t make sense
  • Event accept/decline icons confusing

Low Fidelity Wireframes

From paper to low fi wireframes, the iteration was pretty dramatic.

On the home screen, I removed the tab bar and replaced it with a filter for scheduled/unscheduled events to help simplify the information architecture. I went back to the standard + sign in the bottom corner for the easy event creation for Jill. I included an Archive of past events to help our persona create events from past groups.

For the event chat flow, the major update was the streamlined user flow for a 1 click to land into the chat. I also included the ability to accept the invite from the specific event screen.

For the ETA flow, I created a simple dialog box when setting up the app to explain what the feature is and ease privacy concerns. With the new Auto ETA, I wanted the ability for users to change that setting to be on/off as well as change the amount of time before it would allow ETA to be shared. In the event details, I included an ETA opt out at the request of our client.

The researcher conducted 2 usability tests and found:

  • Enable ETA permissions dialog box is unclear
  • ‘Archive’ and ‘Alerts’ terminology was confusing
  • Unclear that tapping the event card in chat leads to event details

Solution

Visual Design

For the Cue brand, I created a style guide by researching Material Design standards since my client requested an Android-first design. For the primary logo, I wanted to continue to invoke the idea of events and chatting while incorporating more of the Cue brand. So I created a chat bubble from the C in Cue’s secondary logo and added a calendar icon. For the color palette, I updated the green theme from our client but used a much lighter shade to help grab users attention. I found a bright blue color for a secondary color using Material Design’s recommended palettes. Following those standards, I chose the Android Roboto typeface and iconography for the app.

High Fidelity Wireframes

With the style guide complete, I applied the designs to the new Cue app. I started with the sign in process that our client wanted to see with the brand treatment.

After that, I created the permissions screens for contacts, location and send/receive text messages. Underneath the permission, I included the reason the permission was necessary and included a progress indicator for how many permissions the user has left. The language in the Location permission was updated to clarify ETA acronym confusion.

For the high fidelity prototype, I used the secondary blue color for any clickable buttons and links throughout the app. In response to usability testing, I used a History instead of an Archive icon and used a notifications bell icon. In the third event screen, I added the See Event action so that it was obvious that they were clickable. I also included quick information about the event to help Jill get those details quickly. In the final screen, I added feedback for the user that the event has been created.

In the Event Chat flow, a user clicks “See Event” to navigate to the specific event. There, the user can accept events and chat with attendees.

For the Auto-ETA feature, users are able to update it globally in their Settings, including the length of time shared prior. In the event details page, they can also turn it off locally per event.

Usability Findings

We received the following final usability test findings:

  • Participants unable to invite attendees - In response to our client’s usability, I created an ability for participants to be able to invite other people on the event creation screen.
  • Developer feedback - Our client also wanted the ability for users to be able to leave feedback for the developer. Clicking the hyperlink would send them to their mail client to email the company email address.
  • Unable to know who created event or see event attendance status - Our researcher conducted 2 high-fidelity usability tests. We received some usability feedback about wanting more event data in the card so I updated the card to show who created the event as well as whether you accepted the event.
  • ETA language confusing - The ETA language was confusing in event details since it is a double negative. I updated to be a straightforward On/Off toggle.

Reflections

Next Steps

  • For our next steps, I would continue usability testing of our prototype and review results with the client.
  • I’d also want to review potential features that were identified as persona and client needs including: calendar app integration, mobile website preview of Cue event, text updates for non Cue users

What I Learned

  • Importance of balancing user needs with client asks
